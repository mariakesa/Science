For the session you loaded, the dataset is organized **lick by lick**. There are **1851 lick-centered observations**, and for each lick you have a facial movement embedding with **500 Face PCs** plus simultaneously recorded neural activity from **M2 (73 neurons), OFC (72), and OLF (66)**. That matches the paper’s setup: all streams are aligned to individual licks using a 200 ms window centered on each lick. 

The behavioral table looks like it is describing the mouse’s progress through **foraging bouts**. A bout here is one visit to one resource site: the mouse licks several times, then eventually leaves and switches. Your columns strongly support that interpretation: `first lick` marks bout start, `last lick` marks bout end, `reward` marks rewarded licks, `trial number` is effectively a **bout index**, and `consecutive failures` tracks how many unrewarded licks have accumulated in a row within the bout. This lines up with the paper’s behavioral framing, where mice use variables like consecutive failures to decide whether a lick is the last one in the bout. 

The NaNs do **not** look like random corruption. They look structural. The first valid bout starts around row 13, where time becomes defined, `trial number` becomes 1, and `first lick` turns on. That suggests some early rows are padding, startup, or rows where the behavioral bookkeeping is not yet valid. The fact that only `time all licks` and `consecutive failures` contain NaNs, while the other indicator columns do not, also suggests these are variables that are only defined in certain valid task states.

The most interesting exploratory result came from the **CCA** between face and neural activity. You found strong shared linear structure between facial movement and all three regions, but the coupling was strongest for **Face–M2**, weaker for **Face–OFC**, and weakest for **Face–OLF**. In plain language, that means the facial latent space and M2 population activity are varying together more tightly across licks than the face varies with OFC or olfactory cortex. That is nicely consistent with the paper’s story that M2 is especially involved in generating facial expressions of the latent decision variables: the paper reports that decision-variable representations emerge earlier in M2 than in the face, and that M2 inactivation reduces facial decoding accuracy and increases latency.  

So the exploratory takeaway is:

This session appears to contain a clean lick-aligned multimodal record of behavior, face, and cortex during the foraging task. The behavioral annotations reflect bout structure and within-bout evidence accumulation. The missing values seem structured rather than accidental. And at the representational level, facial movements share substantial low-dimensional structure with neural activity, with the strongest alignment showing up in M2, which fits the paper’s mechanistic interpretation.

The one caution is that this is still **exploratory** and apparently based on a single session so far. The CCA numbers are suggestive, not final proof, because some of the shared structure could reflect common task timing, reward/failure patterns, or movement-related variables rather than purely latent cognition. The next step would be to repeat the same analysis across all sessions and then test whether the Face–M2 alignment remains strongest after controlling for obvious behavioral confounds.
